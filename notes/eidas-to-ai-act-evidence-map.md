# eIDAS to AI Act evidence map

Purpose: explain why Cassandra matters beyond trusted-list XML while preserving the project boundary.

## Bridge claim

Cassandra uses eIDAS-adjacent public trusted-list artifacts as a concrete governance-record case. The transferable method is evidence infrastructure: dated observation, provenance, hashing, packaging, verification receipts, dashboard display, and explicit separation between evidence integrity and legal interpretation.

## Transferable evidence-infrastructure needs

| Need in public governance records | Cassandra demonstration | AI Act evidence-infrastructure analogue |
|---|---|---|
| Dated source capture | Saved LOTL-derived snapshot manifests and metadata | Dated AI governance records, registry entries, or conformity evidence snapshots. |
| Stable artifact identity | SHA-256 hashes for sources, manifests, bundles, receipts | Hashes for evidence records, model-card snapshots, risk-management documents, or audit logs. |
| Reproducible comparison | Normalized structural diffs and aggregate telemetry | Drift/change telemetry for governance records, not model behavior claims. |
| Evidence packaging | MIRROR-style bundle and EATF/AEP package loop | Portable evidence capsules for audits and review. |
| Verifier boundary | EATF verifies package bytes and declared hashes only | Verifier confirms evidence package integrity, not legal compliance. |
| Public readability | Static dashboard and schema with caveats | Public or regulator-facing evidence dashboards with non-claim rails. |

Boundary reminder: the transfer path is not legal status determination, source signature validation, supervision, compliance judgment, or public alerting.

## Non-transfer claims

Cassandra does not claim that trusted-list observation directly solves AI Act compliance. It does not evaluate AI systems, model risk, conformity, provider obligations, or legal approval. The connection is methodological: governance records need evidence integrity before they can support accountable discussion.
